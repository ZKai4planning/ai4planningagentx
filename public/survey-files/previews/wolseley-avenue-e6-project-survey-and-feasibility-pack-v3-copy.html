4. Current Known Design Direction From the Survey and Follow-Up Discussion

The property is already close to the target bedroom count conceptually, and the follow-up discussion suggests the owner may not need to force an over-aggressive ground-floor reconfiguration to achieve a viable five-bedroom arrangement now.

The upstairs bathroom / WC solution appears to have become more achievable than first feared, which materially changes the logic for what must happen downstairs.

The current strongest recommended direction is to keep and refit the downstairs bathroom rather than sacrificing it too early for an extra small room that may weaken the scheme overall.

The old kitchen should likely become a bedroom, with the rear kitchen formed as an extension, but the exact extension depth should be tested rather than assumed.

The owner’s more aggressive instinct to maximise extension length and to remove fireplaces / chimneys may create cost, structural, sequencing, and unknown-condition risks that need to be called out properly rather than treated as neutral choices.

Key framing point for the proposal: The team is not simply offering “a drawing.” The team is offering two routes with different implications: a recommended route based on practicality and cost logic, and a client-led route that may chase maximum space at higher complexity and risk.

5. Option Strategy To Be Presented

5A. Option 1 — Recommended Route

Retain and refit the downstairs bathroom rather than deleting it.

Convert the old kitchen into a bedroom.

Create the rear kitchen extension as a strong communal kitchen space, but align the extension more efficiently with the outside back line of the current bathroom structure rather than pushing unnecessarily to the full longest line if that extra depth adds cost without proportional functional gain.

Use the newly workable upstairs sanitary arrangement to reduce pressure on the downstairs reconfiguration.

Present this option as the route that aims to balance useability, compliance, buildability, and cost discipline.

5B. Option 2 — Client-Led / Maximised Route

Show the longer rear extension concept around the circa six-metre line where relevant to the client’s stated ambition.

Show the more aggressive internal-space strategy the owner appears to have in mind, including any stronger push toward fireplace / chimney removal if that is part of the preferred route.

Label this clearly as the more intervention-heavy route, not as the neutral baseline.

Call out that this route carries more unknowns, more exposure to hidden-condition risk, and potentially materially higher cost and programme implications.

5C. Presentation Principle

Do not present both options as equal without commentary.

Option 1 should read as the professionally recommended route.

Option 2 should read as the owner’s more expansive or more aggressive route, to be explored with eyes open rather than endorsed without qualification.

6B. Drawing Rules

Mark every room clearly and consistently. Do not leave ambiguous unnamed spaces if they are central to the option logic.

Show dimensions that matter to decision-making, especially tight rooms, bathroom zones, circulation widths, and the extension depth.

Show windows, doors, stairs, chimney breasts, and current / proposed sanitary positions clearly.

If a fireplace or chimney is being removed in an option, annotate that as a proposal only, not as an assumed easy action.

If future loft access is only being safeguarded rather than built now, show that as a future allowance or future consideration, not as current scope unless instructed otherwise.

Do not make up missing dimensions. Flag anything uncertain as to-be-confirmed.

Do not clean up the drawing in a way that hides awkward geometry, narrow widths, or problem areas. The drawing should support decision-making, not beautify uncertainty.

6C. Specific Items the CAD drawers must not miss

Downstairs bathroom retained in Option 1.

Old kitchen becoming a bedroom in Option 1.

Rear kitchen as extension rather than trying to force too much communal use into the wrong internal footprint.

Exact relative position of the bathroom back wall, because this affects the Option 1 extension line.

All visible chimneys / chimney breasts because they materially affect both room shape and cost logic.

The likely tighter / narrower rooms that may become compliance pinch points.

Stair alignment, landing logic, and whether future loft access has been protected sensibly without compromising current room function.

7. Written Client Proposal Blueprint — What the proposal document should say

7A. Recommended structure

Project understanding — what the owner appears to want and what the survey / follow-up discussion has clarified.

Current property observations — concise summary of what the survey suggests about layout potential and constraints.

Option 1 — recommended route, with short explanation of why it is stronger.

Option 2 — owner-led / more aggressive route, with clear note on complexity, unknowns, and likely cost exposure.

Planning / HMO / compliance next steps — what needs proper checking before any firm commitment.

Refurbishment / construction next steps — only at a budgetary and staged-advice level for now.

Commercial next step — proceed to measured option development / further design / compliance review before any fixed price or full build commitment.

7B. Tone of the written proposal

Businesslike and clear.

No exaggerated certainty.

No mention of internal tools or experimental support methods.

No internal commentary about who liked or did not like previous advisers.

No assumptions dressed up as facts.

7C. Commercial rule for the written proposal

Do not issue fixed prices at this stage.

Only issue budgetary guidance or a broad estimate range if needed, and only with clear caveats.

Split professional service scope from refurbishment scope.

Planning / compliance / drawing work should be one path; construction delivery should be another path.

8. Planning / HMO / Compliance Blueprint

The property must be reviewed against the intended occupancy and use pattern, not just against room labels on a drawing.

Room sizes, sanitary provision, escape logic, fire precautions, and the practical functioning of the shared arrangement must all be tested together.

The planning route and the HMO / licensing route must not be treated as the same thing. They are related, but they are separate decisions.

Article 4 / planning restrictions, room size compliance, bathroom count, kitchen adequacy, fire strategy implications, and any extension planning route should all be left as review items until properly tested.

Practical instruction: In the proposal, frame compliance as “to be tested properly against the chosen option,” not as already achieved.

9. Site Notes to Be Carried Forward Into the Drawing and Proposal Stage

The measured site walk and the original crib-sheet logic should remain the factual base. Do not drift away from the actual measured evidence and the original project understanding.

The measurements table at the beginning of the original crib sheet remains an important reference structure and should continue to inform any room schedule or CAD checking.

Any known discussion points from the site walk about drainage, boiler position, manhole location, chimney count, party wall implications, and potential bathroom positioning should be reflected in the internal note and drafting instructions rather than lost between documents.

The follow-up call added strategic direction, but it did not remove the need to keep unknowns visible.

10. Unknowns, Risks, and What Must Not Be Guessed

Exact final bedroom compliance for the tighter rooms.

Whether any proposed sanitary arrangement works cleanly from a drainage and buildability point of view.

Whether any proposed fireplace or chimney removal is truly justified, safe, and economical once opened up.

Whether the longer extension route genuinely adds value proportionate to its cost and complexity.

Whether the owner’s preferred route remains the same once the options are shown properly.

Exact construction pricing before drawings, scope freeze, and further checks.

Any structural assumptions not yet verified by proper review.

Control rule: If something is not measured, checked, or evidenced, mark it as unconfirmed rather than smoothing over it.
